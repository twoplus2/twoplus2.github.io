Is Australian Federal Politics More Volatile Than Westminster Politics?
 
In the recent EU referendum held in the UK, elements of Australia's immigration system were held up as a solution to the perceived issues with the UK's own system. In future instalments of this series I will aim to test those assumptions but as a warm up to that I aim to test some other assumptions made about Australian politics.
 
 
Australia's recent history of Prime Ministers being removed while in office gives the impression that Australia is an unstable democracy. As Australia is a Westminster parliamentary system where the head of government is elected by the governing party, it seems most relevant to compare Australia's record of Prime Ministerial terms to that of the United Kingdom of Great Britain and Northern Ireland.
 
 
Australia became a self-governing federation in 1901 so we use data for both Australia and the UK from 1901 to 2015. For each year we count the number of Prime Ministers appointed and the number of national elections which took place in that year. If the same Prime Minister is appointed to non-contiguous terms each term will count. For 1901 to 2015 we find the number of PM changes to be -

So on a preliminary basis the two look very evenly matched. As Australia made a significant change to their electoral system and moved to compulsory voting in 1924 we will restrict our analysis to 1924 onwards -

Both countries have had the same number of PM changes between 1924 and 2015. This is very surprising as the two systems differ in their voting system, party systems and most importantly the length of their governmental terms.
 
 
The executive branch of the Australian government is elected on three year terms while in the UK it is elected every five years. However both systems allowed the government to call an election earlier, until the UK's Fixed-term Act of 2011. Looking at the number of elections called we find -

We can see that although Australia had 52% more elections during the same period both countries had the same number of changes of Prime Minister. On a per election basis -

Very roughly we can say that the UK changes Prime Minister 96% of the time at an election while Australia changes Prime Minister only 63% of the time.
 
 
Let's look at that over time. Using the PM Change to Election ratio to adjust for the different terms this plot shows the PM changes to election ratio with a rolling 5 year window -

For Australia there are some obvious spikes around the World Wars and the 1970s. For the UK the spikes are lower but more consistent. If we smooth the data by taking a ten year mean -
 
Prime Minister Changes per Election 10 Year Mean
 
Australia still has the same spikes but a linear regression shows both the UK and Australia with declining trends. On the basis of this both countries have been relatively evenly matched over the last two decades with a slight up-tick in the last five years. If we look at the volatility of each -
 
Prime Minister Changes per Election 5 Year Rolling Standard Deviation
 
Both countries have had periods of volatility with Australia experiencing more volatility over World War II and the post-war period. Largely though both are historically very stable and the recent volatility is elevated but not significantly so.
 
Is Australian Federal Politics More Volatile Than Westminster Politics?
No. The two are very similar and Australia has been as stable if not more stable considering the difference in the number of elections.
